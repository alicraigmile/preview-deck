Open and closed circulatory systems

  • Living cells absorb nutrients and oxygen, as well as release waste products from the body
  • Some animals, such as insects, have an open circulatory systemwhere blood flows freely through their body cavity, carrying nutrients to their cells
  • Oxygen is delivered directly to the insect’s tissues through tiny tubes that open on the outside
  • Vertebrates have a closed system in which the blood flows in arteries, veins and capillaries

Single and double circulatory systems

  • Fish have a single circulatory system in which blood travels from the heart to the gills and then on to the body where it absorbs oxygen and releases carbon dioxide
  • Mammals have a double circulation where blood passes through the heart twice on every loop of the body
  • Blood passes from the heart to the lungs
  • Blood passes from the heart to the organs and tissues in the body, and back to the heart

The heart

  • Blood enters the heart throughveins where it is at its lowest pressure
  • Blood leaves the heart in arterieswhere it is at its highest pressure
  • The systolic pressure is the pressure in the arteries when the heart is contracting, and the diastolic pressure is when the heart’s chambers are refilling with blood

Controlling heart beat

  • The pulse is a measure of heart beat
  • The heart’s action can be investigated using electrocardiograms (ECGs) and echocardiograms
  • Pacemakers are muscles in the heart that regulate the contractions; two groups of pacemaker cells coordinate the contractions of the heart muscles
  • Impulses from the SAN (sino-atrial node) cause the atria to contractand stimulate the atrio-ventricular node and impulses from the AVN cause the ventricles to contract

Blood circulation

  • Through animal dissection the Roman doctor Galen understood the importance of the pulse and how blood moved around the body
  • William Harvey was a doctor in London who explained blood circulates from the heart to the lungs, and from the heart to the rest of the body
  • A single circulatory system (such as in fish) links to a two-chambered heart
  • A double circulatory system (such as in humans) links to a four-chambered heart
